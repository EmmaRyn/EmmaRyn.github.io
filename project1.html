Project Definition

The Problem

Here's The Scoop

There are so many sources to get recipes like websites, friends, family, meal subscription services and cookbooks. This allows recipes to come in many different formats and say you want to get your Mom’s Mac & Cheese recipe - where do you go? Check your email? Dig through a drawer? There needs to be a common place to store all sort of recipes.

This is where Heirloom comes in.

But with a cloud recipes application comes some challenges:

  • Competition

    Since there are so many storage applications out there, I really needed to design one that would make users choose this one and still meet the standards of existing apps.

  • Complexity and Flexibility

    A recipe on a website has cook time, servings, nutrition, ingredients, directions and images all laid out. A lot of this information isn’t immediately available if it’s a family recipe or you haven’t made it yet. This application needs to be able to hold all the important information without relying on it all being available on creation.

  • Simplicity

    Recipe sites that currently exist all have a lot going on. This application needed to be designed so that it was able to do everything that a recipe application needed to do without being overwhelming and difficult to use.

The Solution

  • Enter recipes at whatever pace or level of detail you want
  • Choose whether you want to upload a recipe card or enter ingredients and directions manually
  • Organize recipes into collections when you add them or save that for later
  • Search by ingredients or tags to make finding a cake recipe a piece of cake
  • Share recipes with friends and family but keep them private so you can keep the secret ingredient a secret

Competitive Analysis

I did a SWOT analysis of Dropbox, Pinterest and Yummly which helped me find things I want to mimic and things I want to fix in my own application.

  • Dropbox
  • Strengths
    Simple branding
    Ability to access to files in many ways
    Collaboration Abilities
    Easy to navigate
  • Weaknesses
    Lack of customizations
    Lack of personality
    Desktop app not as feature-rich as mobile app
  • Pinterest
  • Strengths
    Customization of pages
    Many ways to organize content
    Many ways to share content
    Browser extension available
  • Weaknesses
    Adding a pin is not in top menu
    Hard to differentiate ads and friends' pins
    No "go-to-top" button when scrolling
  • Yummly
  • Strengths
    Save or share recipes
    Create a shopping list off of recipe
    Organize recipes into collections
    Education on cooking techniques
  • Weaknesses
    Cannot filter or sort recipes
    Cooking techniques are combined with other articles
    Recipe directions are difficult to read

User Stories

From the survey and many iterations of testing,I was given a lot of feedback on features people would like to see in a recipes application.

I needed to sort through all these and determine what is mandatory for the Minimum Viable Product, what would be great to have and what can be in another round of this application’s development.

As a user, I want to
  • upload pictures of recipes
  • enter recipes manually
  • save recipes from the web
  • add nutritional information to a recipe
  • share a recipe with a link that can be copy and pasted
  • create a collection to group recipes
  • search recipes by ingredient
  • edit a recipe
  • add tags to a recipe

Branding

Determining the font styles and the primary branding colors greatly improved the efficiency of creating the mockups. It gave me guidelines to begin my work with. Once I could see how those branding decisions affected the designs and prototype testing, then I was able to tweak here and there. I ended up removing the darkest color from the styleguide as that was never used, I tweaked the icons and removed the logos that seemed like they would never be utilized.

I also spent some time on logo design. The shape of the tomato changed. Then I played with coloring and brand fonts. Then I tried some different text placement. I ended with this logo which I am pretty happy with as it is my first logo design.

High-Fidelity Mockups

Round One

The first mockup I created attempted to mimic an actual recipe card. The feedback from the usability testing that most greatly influenced the final design was how different the “add recipe” screens were from the “view recipe” screens. Being able to visualize the end-result when creating the recipe is extremely important. Overall, the process of adding a recipe could be simplified and the hierarchy of the home page also needed some adjustment.

This is the first high-fidelity mockup of the recipe page. I took the wireframe and made it look more similar to a real recipe card. I also reduced screens by having more information on one tab instead of multiple.

This is the first high-fidelity mockup of adding ingredients to a recipe. The wireframe version did not have visibility into the process of adding a recipe which caused confusion. This version shows the user all the steps of adding a recipe.

Round Two

Using the feedback from the design critique, I created a second hi-fi mockup. It kept similar styling but laid out the information on the page in a different way. The “view recipe” screen more closely matched the “add recipe” screens, but the design was still just a little too bland and the process of adding a recipe needed some rework. Users needed to be guided through the process and didn’t know where to begin adding a recipe.

This mockup organizes the information in a layout that shows more information clearly with less buttons. All the font-sizes were reduced and the layout was slightly modernized.

This version of adding a recipe keeps everything on one page instead of different tabs. It also makes adding ingredients and directions easier.

Round Three

The third mockup was a lot cleaner. The “add” and “view” recipe screens matched exactly and users had a much easier time adding a recipe. They knew where to begin and felt like this would be extremely useful when actually cooking. The one thing that lacked in this version was the branding. It was missing the “homey” and “rustic” feel that the home page has. It was also missing some information that users were asking for like Cook Time and Servings.

This mockup came after testing a few variations of layouts using a preference test. I used the most liked parts of each layout and turned them into this mockup. It is similar to the last mockup but this one presents the information in a customizable card layout.

The card layout makes it more obvious that all the information is optional. This also keeps the screen cleaner if the user does not have all the information like the nutrition data or an image. The area to add ingredients and directions was moved to the bottom of the panels so it more closely reflects how you add items to a list.

Round Four

The final mockup added the missing information, a “Print Recipe” icon and adjusted some font and icon sizing. The designs were all updated to make sure that branding was consistent and clean.

This is the final mockup of the recipe page. It is the same as the last mockup, but adds more color and also some missing information like cook time and number of servings.

This is the final high-fidelity mockup of adding a recipe.

Conclusion

Recipes are more complicated than I thought, but all the users who tested were excited to use this application. I learned a lot about the importance of simplicity, consistency and hierarchy when designing an application. Also, finding inspiration from existing sites and designs is key for having a design that matches modern trends.
