Project Definition

The Problem

What Is Rating
In the transportation industry, there is a process called “Rating” which is putting a quote from a carrier on a load. This quote can be retrieved many ways, one of which is manual entry.

There are a lot of configurations that can be applied to a manual rating screen, such as charge-level currency, charge-level references or distributing a charge amongst many loads.

  • Here is an Example:

  • I am shipping 100 water bottles. I need someone to carry them from Raleigh to San Diego.

  • I call up this carrier that I know does a good job. He says "I will do it for $500 plus 2 cents for every mile to cover fuel."

  • I enter in his carrier information and the rate he provided and put it onto my load of 100 water bottles.

The Solution

  • Make the form quick and easy to submit
  • Make it easy to add charges
  • Hide secondary details
  • Use new MercuryGate design system

Research & Discovery

UX Audit

There was a lot learning ahead of me. My first step to understanding was learning what Rating is. This included external research as well as reading internal documentation.

Then I looked at the existing screens within the system and played with them to help me understand the current interaction.

All of this external and internal research helped me learn the relevant terminology and customer use cases which would be important for communicating with stakeholders.

This is a slightly less old version of the manual rating screen.

Stakeholder Interviews

I spoke to internal stakeholders such as Product Managers, Business Solutions Architect and Implementation Specialists to learn about how customers use Manual Rating now. What makes the current process difficult?

Design Research

I did a search on google and dribbble for some inspiration. I looked for invoices, receipts, rates and quotes to understand how charges and totals are written and read.

Design

Carrier Information

There were two areas of information that I tackled separately. The top area of carrier data was just a matter of organizing the fields in a way they make sense. I put all possible required fields on the left since that would be the first area a user would look at. When organizing the rest of the panel, I looked at data length and the relationship between other fields into consideration.

Rate Table

When designing the rate table, I lined the input fields up in a way that it read like a sentence. One of the problems with the existing screen was trying to put too much information on one line and cluttering the page with less important details. During the redesign, I prioritized the information and went through many iterations on how to expose the primary information.

Secondary Charge Details

Anything that was secondary information, was only exposed when a charge line was expanded. This helped the page remain simple and readable.

I went through many iterations of these designs, reviewing with the UX team and the stakeholders.

Implementation

Ready for Grooming

When a development team was ready to pick this up, the Product Owner mentioned some Rating features that had not been mentioned up until then. This included charge-level currency and assigning charges to a specific stop. I quickly had to add in additional fields and re-work some of the charge rows to add in more information. We did some quick review sessions on these designs and then implementation was ready to begin.

In Development

A contractor team was in charge of developing this. This was one of my first times directly leading the UX part of an epic. They asked questions about components and I answered. I tried to give lots of feedback as I watched their progress, but as it was a bit rushed, there were some misses.

Conclusion

I learned that more documentation detailing components and spacing is necessary for communicating complex designs, especially with contractor groups who are less familiar with the component system. I also learned that I need to insert myself more into these contractor groups so I can give more immediate feedback, which would decrease the number of open UX issues at the end.
